  title: '[PDF] A First Principles Review of a Novel De Facto Preventive Cybersecurity Strategy'
  authors: Fazal Raheman, Tejas Bhagat, Panayiotis Kotzanikolaou, Javier Gutiérrez Meana, Alfredo Cuzzocrea, Vincent Lefebvre,
    Sandeep Dongre, Anatolly Melnyk, Andrej Grebenc
  abstract: Vulnerabilities in ICT infrastructure largely stem from the design practice of granting third-party permissions
    (TPPs) to software vendors. Our group was the first to demonstrate that TPPs are the root cause of systemic vulnerabilities,
    and that their elimination could enable a new paradigm—Zero Vulnerability Computing (ZVC). Legacy cybersecurity systems
    are reactive, multi-step, and human-driven, allowing vulnerabilities to emerge and then attempting to defend against them.
    This reactive posture contributes to the exponential rise in cybercrime, projected to reach $23.8 trillion by 2027. This
    paper advances the ZVC paradigm, which was tested and validated in our earlier work on hardware wallet experiments under
    the European Commission’s Grant program. We further investigate whether eliminating TPPs at the design phase conflicts
    with the first principles of software engineering. Rather than revalidating efficacy, we adopt a hypothesis-building approach
    supported by the historical deconstruction of computer design rules and previous empirical validation. Our analysis reveals
    that TPPs are recurrent design constructs rather than axiomatic necessities, and that their removal is architecturally
    viable. We evaluate ZVC’s viability and offer a comparative analysis that distinguishes ZVC from legacy Zero Trust models.
    ZVC’s core protocol operates without traditional user-facing cryptography, making it inherently resistant to quantum computing
    threats and capable of addressing approximately 90% of known “structural vulnerabilities.” The remaining 10%, mostly “semantic
    vulnerabilities,” can be mitigated through proactive strategies integrated with artificial intelligence. This review lays
    the foundation for a preventive, quantum-resilient cybersecurity paradigm that is automated, cost-effective, and future-proof.
    We conclude by outlining implementation challenges, open research questions, and future directions.
